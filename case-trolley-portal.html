Selected work Trolley · Fintech

Redesigning how 4 million people get paid

Trolley’s recipient-facing portal was a single scrollable widget with no hierarchy, no home, and no context. This is the research-led redesign that changed that.

Bottom line: Fewer support tickets, fewer silent payment failures, and one consistent experience across portal, widget, and iframe.

Discovery & Research

Five merchant types. One broken experience.

Research spanned five distinct merchant categories, each with different recipient profiles, payout preferences and failure modes. Every one of them shared the same complaints about the portal.

5
Merchant types researched across gaming, creative, contractor, developer and creator economy
4M+
Recipients across 210+ countries relying on the portal for payouts
3
Primary failure categories: tax confusion, verification friction, missing context
01 · Gaming Platform

“Our recipients expect a mobile-first experience, they're used to Cash App and Venmo. A complex web form doesn't match their mental model at all.”

30K recipients · US · 18–30 · Bank transfer
02 · Digital Art Marketplace

“Artists don't know where their money went. They submit bank details and then nothing, no confirmation, no timeline, just silence.”

25K recipients · Global · 22–45 · Mixed PayPal/Bank
03 · EU Contractor Platform

“Our users are sophisticated policy professionals. But the form reads like it was designed for US tax lawyers, not European contractors.”

12K recipients · EU · 30–55 · Bi-weekly
04 · Developer Tools Company

“A contractor switched account type and payments stopped, they had no idea changing that field would invalidate their tax form.”

79K recipients · 50+ countries · 25–44
05 · Influencer Marketing

“Our creators are money-savvy with zero tolerance for friction. They want fast, direct bank transfers, no intermediaries.”

51K recipients · US · 18–34 · $1B+ paid

Across all five, six cross-cutting patterns held: mobile-first always, global ≠ US tax literacy, trust is fragile, bank transfer is winning, every support ticket is a design failure, and context was missing everywhere.

Problem Definition

What was actually broken

Research surfaced a clear gap between what the portal provided and what recipients needed. These weren't aesthetic issues, they were structural failures causing real payment delays and support burden.

1
Everything in one screen, no hierarchy. General info, payout method, bank details, verification, errors and legal disclaimers crammed into one scrollable form. Users didn't know what needed action.
2
Multiple simultaneous errors, no recovery path. Name mismatch, wrong account type and unverified status appeared together with no priority, and “Add & Activate” CTAs showed even in error states.
3
Tax flows with no guidance for non-US users. W-8BEN and W-9 forms appeared with minimal explanation. Global recipients, the majority, had no context for withholding or why they needed a TIN.
4
No portal beyond the widget. No home, no payment history, no notification center. Every interaction started from scratch with no continuity.
5
“Delivered” with no explanation. Status showed “Delivered” but recipients hadn't received funds, driving thousands of support tickets monthly.
6
Account type changes had no impact assessment. Switching Individual→Business silently invalidated tax forms. Users discovered it only when payments stopped.
Design Decisions

How research shaped the design

Each structural decision traces directly to a research finding, not aesthetic preference.

01

From widget → full portal

Finding: recipients had no home. Decision: a full wallet home with payment summary, alert state, notifications and quick actions, and a responsive sidebar that collapses to a thumb-reachable bottom nav.

02

Progressive disclosure for account edits

Finding: switching account type silently broke everything. Decision: a 7-step guided edit flow with an explicit impact warning showing exactly what will change before you commit.

03

One error, one fix

Finding: the old widget showed three errors at once and users froze. Decision: each verification step surfaces a single issue, with an inline FAQ answering “why do I have withholding?” next to the field.

04

Context everywhere

Finding: artists didn’t know why forms were voided; EU contractors didn’t understand withholding. Decision: every form shows status, rate and why, in plain language, not IRS terminology.

Before / After

The old widget vs. the new portal

The before reflects the actual 2024 widget, a single-scroll form with no error hierarchy. The after shows the redesigned step-by-step flows — explore both in the live prototype below.

Before
  • Single scrollable form, all sections, all errors at once
  • Up to 4 conflicting error messages, no hierarchy
  • “Add & Activate” visible even in error states
  • No step indicator, no sense of progress
  • Legal disclaimer always visible, visual noise
After
  • Step indicator shows exactly where the user is
  • One status message per screen, clear colour coding
  • Single CTA: Fix & Retry or Add & Activate
  • Account type handled separately with impact assessment
  • Mobile-first: thumb-reachable primary actions

Outcomes

What changed for recipients

↓ Tax support
Tickets dropped after inline FAQ and contextual error states reduced confusion about withholding and voided forms
↑ Completion
Step-by-step account-type edit flow surfaced impact warnings before destructive changes, fewer silent failures
3 surfaces
Consistent experience across standalone portal, embedded widget and iframe, same logic, adapted layout

The redesign moved from a widget-first to a portal-first model, with research-validated priorities for the flows that caused the most friction.

What I'd do with more time: usability testing with hourly workers and international recipients on the tax flow; an A/B test of the step-by-step edit vs. the old single form; longitudinal tracking of support volume by category; and proactive “your tax form expires in 30 days” notifications before a payment is blocked.
