The context

Fifty years of rights, locked in 85,000 documents.

A music publisher controls the rights to thousands of songs, which means deciding who can use each song and who gets paid for it. Half a century of that knowledge lived in documents, spread across three systems built for storage, not for answering questions.

85K+
Contract PDFs, invoices and royalty rows across Dropbox, FileMaker and Vistex
3
Systems of record with no way to cross-reference one against another
0
Single sources of truth. The full picture lived only in someone's head

Contracts sat in Dropbox (~5,000 PDFs), invoices and quotes in FileMaker (~20,000), and royalties in Vistex (~60,000 rows). Each system answered part of a question and none of them answered a whole one. To get a real answer, someone opened all three and reconciled them by hand.

Discovery & research

Where the real answers actually lived.

I ran interviews across the company and reviewed the three systems and more than 85,000 documents. The same need showed up in every role: a fast answer they could stand behind, pulled from more than one system at once.

A&R

Prepping for an artist meeting means assembling contract terms, end dates, recent royalty performance and sync activity, pulled from three places by hand.

Business development

“Which contracts are coming up for renewal, and which are worth chasing?” There was no calendar, no pipeline, no alerts.

Sync licensing

Quoting a placement is guesswork without history: what did we charge for comparable genres, productions and territories?

Finance & royalties

Reconciling a payment means matching Vistex royalty data back to the contract terms and recoupment status that justify it.

The common thread across every function: one fast answer they could stand behind, reasoned across more than one system at once, with the source attached.

The insight

The hard part was reasoning across systems, and proving it.

One test reframed the whole project. A simple question like “when does this contract expire?” can be answered by any document tool, citing the clause. The question that runs the business day to day is harder.

“How much do we owe this artist right now?” Answering that means cross-referencing the contract's recoupment terms against several quarters of royalty data and working out the unrecouped balance. No single system holds it. The answer has to be reasoned across all three, then proven.

The query that set the bar

So the design problem was not “can it chat with a PDF.” It was making a multi-system answer trustworthy: every figure grounded in its source, scored for confidence, and visible only to the people allowed to see it. That set five principles I specced for the system's behavior.

01

Ground every figure

No answer appears without the exact contract clause, royalty period or ledger line it was drawn from. Grounding at the clause level, not the document summary, so every number is checkable against its source.

02

Score the confidence, gate on it

The system states how sure it is and what it could not reconcile. I set the confidence gate as a product decision: below the threshold, the answer routes to a specialist instead of surfacing.

03

Keep a human in the loop

The system drafts; a person confirms or edits before anything is written to the record. Each correction feeds back to improve retrieval, so the next similar question lands closer.

04

Respect who can see what

Role-based access and an audit trail as design principles, because royalty and contract data is not for everyone, and sensitive answers leave a record.

Product strategy · build vs buy

Eight tools against the real requirements.

The bar was not “can it summarize a PDF.” A tool had to reach all three systems, reason across them, ground answers at the level of a clause or a royalty period, score confidence, and enforce role-based access, all at the cost structure of a small team. That narrowed eight options to a tiered recommendation.

The product

Ask a question, get a cited answer.

The product is a permissioned data layer with a plain-language interface on top. Beyond the direct question and answer, the same engine drives a live business dashboard and an always-on contract calendar.

Confidence gateServe if sure, flag a person if not

This is a retrieval (RAG) workflow with agentic steps and a human gate: it interprets the question, pulls from the connected systems, drafts a grounded answer, scores its own confidence, and either serves the answer with citations or flags exactly what it could not reconcile and routes it to a specialist. A person confirms before anything is saved.

The management dashboard

Catalog revenue, active contracts, recoupment exposure and a live count of renewals due. The read on the business that leadership never had.

The contract calendar

Every deal surfaces itself at 90, 60 and 30 days before expiry, with context on whether it's worth chasing, so renewals stop lapsing.

Self-serve for every team

A&R, sync, finance and biz dev each get the cited, permissioned answer they used to route through management.

Proof on sensitive data

Role-based access and an audit trail on royalty and contract data, plain-language answers that a non-technical team can check.
