How I work with AI.

Most of my work sits on high-stakes systems, the kind where people act on what the interface tells them: payments, identity, clinical care. AI is the newest layer I've folded into that, and it changes the problem. It's fast and useful, and it adds failure modes we didn't have to design for before. The part I design is the layer in between: where the system explains itself, where people can check it, and where a bad answer gets caught before it matters.

Up front: I've shipped two AI products end to end, and AI now runs through how I prototype, synthesize research, and build. What hasn't changed is the judgment underneath it, ten years of product design: the research, the methods, the systems. One tool in the box, not the box.

TL;DR

Designing for AI

When the AI is the product.

A lot of my current work is the AI being the product, not just the assistant. I've shipped two, end to end.

Both sit on high-stakes data, so the hard design problem isn't the interface, it's trust. There's real risk of a confident wrong answer on numbers people are about to act on. I design for it in three layers. Explanation: the system shows its reasoning and where an answer came from. Verification: people can check that answer against the source before they rely on it. Intervention: when something looks off, there's a clear way to catch it before it costs anything. Same three layers on both products, whatever the surface.

01 An agentic RAG decision-support system Ask the Catalog · music publishing

A music publisher had fifty years of song rights buried across three systems that didn't talk to each other. I led the research and end-to-end design of a tool that answers a rights question in plain language and shows the exact contract clause it came from. Under that it runs on retrieval-augmented generation (RAG) with an agentic retrieval flow. It handles the factual lookups and the calls that sit on them, like defensible audit totals and sync licensing. A confidence check decides when the system answers on its own and when it routes to a specialist, and a person signs off before anything is written back to the record.

A routine rights question went from about 30 minutes across four systems to under 2, every answer links back to its source clause, and the company approved the full build after the pilot. Read the full case study →

A reliable data engine plus a model that can drift is a specific kind of problem, and human-in-the-loop checkpoints are usually the answer. Here, conflicting metadata had to be reconciled with a person in the loop, and that pattern transfers to almost any AI product with stakes attached.

02 A clinical conversational AI environment Femtech · non-public

A conversational AI framework built with medical professionals and headed toward clinical use. I ran the UX research, then designed the environment around conversation: the dialogue, the voice, and how the model's behavior gets trained and evaluated, worked out with the clinical team. What someone says reshapes the inputs and updates the dashboard live, so the conversation is the input layer, not a chat window bolted on the side.

Conversation alone wasn't the answer. Open-ended chat is a weak default when people need to act, so I paired it with guided flows for the moments someone needs a specific action, not a blank prompt. It's mixed-initiative by design: people work from a structured plan and lean on the conversation when something's ambiguous, rather than making it the only way through.

The discipline was deciding where AI did not belong. The conversational layer had to earn its place, serving the product and working with the other features instead of running beside them. Knowing where to hold it back is the design work, and on a clinical product, where people act on what the system says, safety sets the bar.

Prototyping

From a brief to a working prototype.

The first thing I hand over is often a rough but working wireframe, not a static concept everyone reads a different way.

I'll move from a brief into a working proof of concept fast, using Claude Code, V0, Figma Make, or Lovable depending on what the project needs, then put it in front of the team so we're all reacting to the same thing instead of the same idea described five different ways. It collapses the distance between “here's what I'm thinking” and “here's what it feels like to use.”

This isn't a rule, it depends on the project. For big, complex systems I still go back to drawn work and Figma to plan the structure first. The tooling moves weekly, so I stay loose about the tool and firm about the workflow, and I keep up by reading Reddit threads and watching what people actually ship, not waiting for the polished writeup.

Design systems

A design system is documentation, not a sticker sheet.

I build it in two layers. A developer-facing layer that lines up with the engineers' repo and Storybook, so they have an exact spec to build from. And a usage layer, closer to how gov.uk documents theirs, with plain guidance on when to use a pattern and when not to: when an inline validation message is the right call and when a page-level banner is, say, so two teams don't solve the same problem two different ways. That usage layer is what keeps a system from drifting, because most gaps come from people not knowing how to apply something, not from a missing component. I structure both so it scales without me having to be in every conversation.

When the documentation is that strong, AI lets me design straight in the environment and even ship from it. It's part of why I care about building the system early rather than treating it as cleanup, since a good system is what makes the fast prototyping above actually hold up.

Research & synthesis

Where I still do the legwork.

I recently ran about twenty interviews and used AI heavily to synthesize and organize them. The important part: it does not do the legwork for me. I do that. What it gives me is structure and a clean document to build on, and I work through it section by section rather than trusting the first pass.

Once the pieces exist, I'll have it pull my separate documents into one cohesive thing, same look and feel, so a client gets a single coherent artifact instead of a pile of notes. Sometimes that becomes a small website I spin up for them.

Building

I build the things I use.

My project dashboard, the one I run my own work through, I built with Claude Code. My portfolio site is hand-built HTML I generate and deploy straight to Netlify, no Squarespace, and when I want to change something I'll regenerate the whole thing from scratch. I even built a chatbot for the portfolio, the ITA bot, so people can ask it questions about my work directly.

I'm not pointing at this to show off the tools. It's the proof that I'd rather understand a system by building it than admire it from the outside. Reading the actual repos, and building my own tools, means I design against real constraints instead of hypothetical ones, and work as a real partner to engineers rather than throwing designs over a wall.

That's the short version. AI runs through nearly every stage of how I work now, from pricing a project to shipping a prototype, but the judgment underneath it is still the ten years of design. Happy to walk through any of this live and show you the actual screens.
